## Chapter 1: What Is Low-FODMAP and Why Your Child Needs It

You're here because your child's tummy hurts. Maybe it's a dull ache after dinner. Maybe it's a sudden cramp that sends them running to the bathroom during math class. Maybe it's an unexplained bloating that makes them look six months pregnant by mid-afternoon. Maybe it's the tears, the missed school days, the cancelled playdates.

You've been to the pediatrician. Maybe you've seen a specialist. You've heard words like "irritable bowel syndrome" and "functional abdominal pain" and "we're not sure what's causing it." You've tried cutting out dairy, then gluten, then sugar. Nothing stuck. Nothing helped consistently.

The Low-FODMAP diet is different. It's not a random elimination based on a hunch. It's a science-backed, clinically validated protocol developed by researchers at Monash University in Australia specifically for people with sensitive digestive systems. And here's the good news: it works for 70-86% of people with IBS.

### What Are FODMAPs?

FODMAP is an acronym. It stands for:

**F**ermentable **O**ligosaccharides, **D**isaccharides, **M**onosaccharides **A**nd **P**olyols

That's a mouthful, so let's break it down. FODMAPs are types of carbohydrates — sugars and fibers — that are naturally present in many foods. In most people, these carbohydrates are absorbed normally in the small intestine. But in people with sensitive guts (like many children with IBS), these carbohydrates don't get absorbed properly. Instead, they travel to the large intestine where they:

1. **Pull in water** — causing diarrhea or loose stools
2. **Get fermented by gut bacteria** — producing gas, bloating, and pain

It's not an allergy. It's not an intolerance in the traditional sense. It's a mechanical issue — the gut can't handle these specific carbohydrates efficiently.

### Why Children Are Different

Children's digestive systems are still developing. Their guts are more permeable, their microbiome is less stable, and their nervous systems are more reactive. This means a child with IBS doesn't just experience the physical symptoms — they experience the anxiety of not knowing when their stomach will hurt, the embarrassment of urgent bathroom trips in front of their peers, and the social isolation of being "the kid who can't eat anything."

The Low-FODMAP diet gives children something powerful: **predictability**. When you know which foods trigger symptoms, you can plan meals with confidence. You can pack IBS-friendly school lunches without fear. You can cook **Low-FODMAP kids' meals** that won't lead to tears an hour later.

### What This Diet Is (and What It Isn't)

**The Low-FODMAP diet is:**
- A temporary elimination protocol (typically 2-6 weeks)
- A structured reintroduction process to identify your child's specific triggers
- A personalized long-term eating plan based on what your child can tolerate
- A tool backed by 20+ years of clinical research

**The Low-FODMAP diet is NOT:**
- A lifelong sentence to restrictive eating
- A one-size-fits-all diet (every child tolerates different foods)
- A weight loss diet
- A cure for IBS (there's currently no cure — but symptoms can be managed)

### What to Expect from This Book

This book is organized around one central promise: **your child can eat delicious, satisfying food that doesn't hurt.** We've created 100+ **tummy-safe recipes** that have been tested by real kids and approved by picky eaters. These aren't bland, sad meals. They're pancakes, pizza, cookies, mac and cheese (yes, really), and birthday cake — all redesigned to be gentle on your child's digestive system.

In the chapters ahead, you'll learn:
- Exactly which foods contain FODMAPs and which don't (Chapter 8)
- How to implement the 3-phase protocol safely (Chapter 3)
- How to help your picky eater embrace new foods (Chapter 6)
- How to pack school lunches, navigate birthday parties, and eat out (Chapter 7)
- How to meal plan so you're not cooking separate dinners (Part 8)

Your child's IBS doesn't define them, and it doesn't have to control your family's meals. Let's take the first step together.

## Chapter 2: Is This Diet Safe for Kids?

This is the most important question a parent can ask, and the answer deserves a straight, honest response.

**Yes, the Low-FODMAP diet is safe for children when done correctly — but it must be done under medical supervision.**

### The Short Answer

The Low-FODMAP diet was originally developed for adults with IBS, but it has been increasingly studied and used in pediatric populations. Research published in the Journal of Pediatric Gastroenterology and Nutrition and other peer-reviewed journals has demonstrated that the Low-FODMAP diet can significantly reduce abdominal pain, bloating, and other IBS symptoms in children ages 6-18.

A 2020 study found that 76% of children with IBS who followed a Low-FODMAP diet reported significant improvement in their symptoms. Those are numbers that rival or exceed many pharmaceutical interventions.

### The Responsible Approach

Here is the correct way to implement the Low-FODMAP diet for a child:

**Step 1: Get a proper diagnosis.** Before starting any elimination diet, your child should have a confirmed diagnosis of IBS or a related functional gastrointestinal disorder from a pediatrician or pediatric gastroenterologist. This rules out other conditions like celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or food allergies that require different treatments.

**Step 2: Work with a dietitian.** A registered dietitian who specializes in pediatric gastrointestinal disorders can help you implement the diet safely, ensure your child is meeting all nutritional requirements, and guide the reintroduction phase. Many insurance plans cover dietitian visits for diagnosed IBS.

**Step 3: Monitor growth and development.** Children need a wide range of nutrients to support their growing bodies. During the elimination phase, some high-FODMAP foods that are also sources of important nutrients (like certain fruits, vegetables, and dairy) are temporarily restricted. A dietitian can help you find alternative sources and ensure your child isn't missing anything essential.

**Step 4: Do not let the elimination phase go on too long.** The low-FODMAP elimination phase is meant to last 2-6 weeks — not months or years. The goal is to identify triggers, not to permanently restrict foods. Prolonged unnecessary restriction can lead to nutritional deficiencies, disordered eating patterns, and unnecessary stress.

### When NOT to Start the Low-FODMAP Diet

Do not start this diet if:

- Your child is already underweight or losing weight
- Your child has a diagnosed eating disorder or a history of food restriction anxiety
- Your child is going through a major life stressor (starting a new school, family move, divorce)
- You have not yet ruled out celiac disease with proper testing (celiac requires gluten in the diet for accurate testing)

### Common Parent Concerns

**"Will this give my child an unhealthy relationship with food?"**

This is a valid concern. Labeling foods as "good" and "bad" can be problematic, especially for children. The key is framing: Low-FODMAP foods aren't "good" foods, and high-FODMAP foods aren't "bad" foods. High-FODMAP foods are simply foods that trigger your child's specific symptoms. We teach this to children using the traffic light system in Chapter 4 — it's about how your body responds, not a moral judgment.

**"What if my child refuses to eat Low-FODMAP foods?"**

This is addressed in depth in Chapter 6. The short version: involve your child in cooking, offer choices, and don't force it. A hungry child usually eats. A pressured child usually resists.

**"Will my child miss out socially?"**

Some birthday parties and school events will be harder. But with preparation (covered in Chapter 7), your child can participate fully. Many parents find that packing a safe "party pack" that's even more exciting than the offered food solves the problem.

### Bottom Line

The Low-FODMAP diet is a legitimate, evidence-based tool for managing pediatric IBS. It is safe when implemented thoughtfully under medical guidance. This book provides the practical resources, recipes, and strategies to make it work for your family.

But please: **talk to your doctor before starting.** Show them this book. Bring them into the process. A good healthcare team makes all the difference.

## Chapter 3: The 3 Phases — Elimination, Challenge, Personalization

The Low-FODMAP diet isn't a single diet — it's a three-phase protocol. This is the most important concept to understand because many parents fail by treating it as a permanent restriction rather than a temporary diagnostic tool.

### Phase 1: Elimination (2-6 Weeks)

**Goal:** Eliminate all high-FODMAP foods and see if symptoms improve.

During this phase, you will cook only from the "green" list (Chapter 8) and the recipes in this book. Your child will eat low-FODMAP foods exclusively.

**What to expect in Week 1:**
- Your child may still have symptoms (it takes time for FODMAPs to clear the system)
- Your child may resist new foods
- You will feel overwhelmed by the food lists and label reading
- You will wonder if this is worth it

**What to expect in Week 2:**
- Symptoms typically begin to improve (if FODMAPs were the trigger)
- You start to find a rhythm with grocery shopping and meal prep
- Your child begins to accept that "this is what we're doing"
- The overwhelm starts to lift

**What to expect in Weeks 3-6:**
- By week 3, most children who will respond to the diet show clear improvement
- You can decide whether to continue to Phase 2 or extend elimination by 1-2 weeks
- If there is NO improvement by week 3, FODMAPs may not be your child's trigger — consult your doctor about next steps

**Important rule for Phase 1:** Do NOT change anything else. Keep medications, routines, and other habits as consistent as possible. If symptoms improve, you need to know it was the diet change, not something else.

### Phase 2: Reintroduction (6-8 Weeks)

**Goal:** Systematically test individual FODMAP groups to identify which ones trigger your child.

This is the phase most parents rush through — and it's the most important one. Without proper reintroduction, you don't know which FODMAPs are actually problems for your child.

**The reintroduction schedule:**

Each FODMAP group is tested separately over 3 days:

| Day | What You Do |
|-----|-------------|
| Day 1 | Serve a small portion of a test food from one FODMAP group |
| Day 2 | Continue eating low-FODMAP — observe for delayed symptoms (up to 48 hours) |
| Day 3 | Continue eating low-FODMAP — observe and note any symptoms |

If no symptoms appear, that FODMAP group is likely safe for your child (though portion size still matters).

If symptoms appear, that group is a trigger. Rest, let symptoms subside completely (usually 2-3 days of low-FODMAP eating), then test the next group.

**Test foods for each FODMAP group:**

| FODMAP Group | Test Food | What It Tests For |
|--------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Fructose | Honey (1 tbsp) | Excess fructose |
| Lactose | Milk (½ cup) | Lactose malabsorption |
| Fructans | Wheat bread (1 slice) | Wheat/gluten sensitivity |
| Fructans | Onion (1 tbsp cooked) | Onion/garlic sensitivity |
| Galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS) | Chickpeas (¼ cup) | Legume sensitivity |
| Polyols | Mushroom (½ cup) | Sugar alcohol sensitivity |
| Polyols | Avocado (¼ whole) | Sorbitol sensitivity |

**Warning signs during reintroduction:**
- Severe pain or cramping = stop testing and return to elimination
- Diarrhea lasting more than 24 hours = stop and consult your doctor
- Your child refuses to continue = this is normal. Take a break. Try again in a week.

### Phase 3: Personalization (Ongoing)

**Goal:** Create a sustainable, varied diet that avoids only your child's specific trigger FODMAPs.

This is where the diet becomes livable. By now you know:
- Which FODMAP groups trigger your child
- How much of each trigger food your child can tolerate before symptoms appear
- Which safe foods your child actually enjoys

**Personalization strategies:**
- For minor triggers: small portions may be fine (your child might tolerate 2 tbsp of onion but not a full serving)
- For major triggers: avoid completely
- Re-test every 6-12 months: children's digestive systems change, and foods that trigger symptoms today may be fine next year

### The Most Common Mistake

Parents stay in Phase 1 (elimination) for months or years. This is unnecessary, restrictive, and potentially harmful. The elimination phase is a diagnostic test, not a lifestyle. Get through it, do the reintroduction work, and build a personalized diet your child can enjoy long-term.

## Chapter 4: The FODMAP Traffic Light System

One of the hardest parts of the Low-FODMAP diet is explaining it to your child. "You can't eat that because it has fermentable oligosaccharides" doesn't work at the dinner table. That's why we created the FODMAP Traffic Light System.

This simple visual framework helps children understand which foods work for their bodies without creating fear or anxiety around food.

### How It Works

**🟢 GREEN LIGHT (Go!) — Low-FODMAP, eat freely**

These foods are low in FODMAPs and should not trigger symptoms. Your child can eat normal portions of these foods without worry.

*Examples: bananas, blueberries, strawberries, oranges, carrots, potatoes, rice, oats, eggs, chicken, beef, fish, hard cheeses, lactose-free milk*

**🟡 YELLOW LIGHT (Caution!) — FODMAP-dependent on portion size**

These foods are tricky. They're low-FODMAP in small amounts but become high-FODMAP in standard servings. Portion control is the key.

*Examples: avocado (up to ⅛ whole), sweet potato (½ cup), corn (½ cob), peas (¼ cup), grapes (½ cup), pineapple (½ cup)*

**🔴 RED LIGHT (Stop!) — High-FODMAP, avoid during elimination**

These foods are high in FODMAPs and commonly trigger symptoms. They should be avoided during Phase 1 (elimination).

*Examples: apples, pears, watermelon, honey, milk, yogurt, onion, garlic, wheat bread (large amounts), beans, lentils, cashews, pistachios*

### Explaining the System to Your Child

Here's a script that works for most families:

*"Your tummy has a special way it works. Some foods are easy for your tummy to handle — those are green-light foods. Some foods are a little harder — those are yellow-light foods, and you can have a small amount. Some foods make your tummy hurt — those are red-light foods, and we're going to give your tummy a break from them for a while."*

**Tips for different age groups:**

- **Ages 3-6:** Use physical cards or magnets in green, yellow, and red. Let your child sort foods into the right category. Make it a game.
- **Ages 7-11:** Involve them in meal planning. "Let's pick three green foods for your lunchbox today." Kids this age love feeling in control.
- **Ages 12+:** Explain the science briefly. Many teens respond well to understanding WHY the diet works — it gives them ownership over their health.

### Visual References in This Book

Every recipe in this book includes the FODMAP rating at the top:

🟢 **Low-FODMAP** — This recipe is safe as written
🟡 **Low-FODMAP at specified portion** — Stick to the serving size noted
🔴 **Contains high-FODMAP option** — But we've included substitution notes

When you flip to the Food Lists in Chapter 8, they're organized by traffic light color. If you laminate the quick-reference card (download at the back of this book), you and your child can check foods at a glance.

### The Psychology of Green-Yellow-Red

Some parents worry that calling foods "red" will make their child afraid to eat. Here's the reframe:

Red doesn't mean "dangerous." Red means "this food is not right for your body right now." Just like a friend who's allergic to peanuts — peanuts aren't bad, they're just not safe for that person's body.

Over time, most children internalize the system and start self-regulating. We've heard from parents whose children proudly announce at a party: "I can't have that — it's a red for me, but I brought a green cupcake!" That's the goal: confidence, not fear.

## Chapter 5: Setting Up Your Low-FODMAP Kitchen

The first few days of the Low-FODMAP diet are the hardest. You're reading labels, second-guessing every ingredient, and mourning the staples you used to buy without thinking. This chapter will help you set up your kitchen so that cooking low-FODMAP becomes second nature within two weeks.

### Step 1: Pantry Reset (Do This First)

You don't need to throw everything away. You need to separate what's useful from what's not.

**Keep (low-FODMAP staples):**

| Category | Keep |
|----------|------|
| Oils & Fats | Olive oil, coconut oil, butter, ghee, vegetable oil, avocado oil |
| Grains | White rice, brown rice, quinoa, oats (certified gluten-free), rice noodles, corn tortillas, gluten-free pasta |
| Canned Goods | Canned tomatoes (check for garlic/onion), coconut milk (canned), tuna, salmon, low-FODMAP broth (check labels) |
| Spices | All dried herbs and spices — but check blends for garlic and onion powder |
| Sweeteners | Sugar, maple syrup, stevia, glucose syrup |
| Baking | Gluten-free all-purpose flour, rice flour, cornstarch, baking soda, baking powder, cocoa powder |
| Nuts & Seeds | Peanuts, walnuts, pecans, macadamia nuts, pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds, chia seeds, flax seeds |
| Vinegars & Sauces | Rice vinegar, balsamic vinegar (1 tbsp), red wine vinegar, tamari (gluten-free soy sauce), fish sauce, mustard (check for garlic) |

**Move to quarantine (high-FODMAP — revisit during reintroduction):**

| Category | Quarantine |
|----------|------------|
| Grains | Wheat flour, regular pasta, bread (unless gluten-free), rye, barley, couscous |
| Canned Goods | Beans, lentils, chickpeas, baked beans |
| Condiments | Onion powder, garlic powder, honey, agave, high-fructose corn syrup, certain salad dressings |
| Nuts | Cashews, pistachios |
| Fruits | Dried fruits, fruit juice concentrates, apples, pears, mangoes, watermelon, stone fruits |

### Step 2: Label Reading 101

Manufacturers add high-FODMAP ingredients to everything. Here's what to scan for on any package:

**Avoid these on ingredients lists:**
- Onion (any form: onion powder, onion salt, onion extract, dehydrated onion)
- Garlic (any form: garlic powder, garlic salt, garlic extract, garlic oil — yes, garlic oil IS high-FODMAP despite what some blogs claim)
- Honey
- High-fructose corn syrup
- Agave nectar
- Chicory root fiber (often in "healthy" snacks and granola bars)
- Inulin (another name for chicory root fiber)
- Apple juice concentrate
- Pear juice concentrate
- Wheat flour (unless the product is certified low-FODMAP portion)
- Milk solids, condensed milk, evaporated milk
- Soy milk (made from whole soybeans)

**Look for these instead:**
- "Garlic-free" or "onion-free" on the label
- "Certified gluten-free" (usually but not always low-FODMAP)
- "Low-FODMAP certified" (increasing number of products carry this)
- Simple ingredient lists with recognizable items

### Step 3: The Low-FODMAP Meal Prep Workflow

The single biggest predictor of success on this diet is meal prep. When you're tired, hungry, and a busy parent, you will not have the energy to read labels and make low-FODMAP choices from scratch. Prepare ahead.

**Sunday Prep Session (60 minutes):**

1. **Cook a grain base:** Make 2-3 cups of rice, quinoa, or gluten-free pasta. Store in the fridge.
2. **Roast vegetables:** Toss carrots, bell peppers, zucchini, and potatoes with oil and herbs. Roast at 400°F for 25 minutes.
3. **Cook a protein:** Grill or bake chicken breasts, ground beef, or tofu. Store for the week.
4. **Make a dressing:** Whisk olive oil, rice vinegar, mustard, and herbs. Store in a jar.
5. **Portion snacks:** Wash and cut carrots, cucumber, bell peppers. Portion grapes and blueberries into snack bags.

With these five things done, you can assemble a low-FODMAP lunch, dinner, or snack in under 10 minutes.

### Step 4: Tools That Help

You don't need a specialty kitchen, but these tools make Low-FODMAP cooking significantly easier:

- **Food scale** — FODMAP content depends on portion size. A kitchen scale takes the guesswork out of yellow-light foods.
- **Label-reading glasses** — Half joking. Low-FODMAP requires reading a lot of tiny text.
- **Good knife** — You'll be prepping more fresh produce and less packaged food.
- **Instant Pot or slow cooker** — Batch cooking is easier when you can set it and forget it.
- **Silicone freezer molds** — Portion and freeze sauces, broths, and purees for quick meals.

### Step 5: The Emergency Meal Plan

Keep ingredients on hand for these five emergency meals — they're what you make when nothing else works:

1. **Rice bowl:** Leftover rice + roasted veggies + protein + olive oil
2. **Scrambled eggs:** Eggs + hard cheese + spinach + potatoes
3. **Gluten-free pasta:** GF pasta + canned tomatoes (no garlic) + protein + basil
4. **Quesadilla:** Corn tortilla + hard cheese + pre-cooked chicken
5. **Smoothie:** Banana + strawberries + lactose-free milk + oats + maple syrup

## Chapter 6: The Picky Eater Survival Guide

You are trying to feed a picky eater on a restricted diet. On paper, this sounds like a nightmare. In practice, it's challenging but doable. This chapter gives you the strategies that actually work — not the well-meaning advice from friends whose kids eat kale.

### Why Picky Eating Gets Worse on a Restricted Diet

Picky eating is often about control. Children have very few things they can control in their lives. Food is one of them. When you suddenly take away familiar foods and replace them with unfamiliar ones, the picky eating intensifies — not because the child is being difficult, but because their sense of control has been threatened.

Add to that:
- Anxiety around new foods (neophobia peaks around ages 2-6)
- Physical discomfort from previous meals (if high-FODMAP foods caused pain, your child may associate all eating with pain)
- Fatigue from a long day (most picky eating happens at dinner, not breakfast)

### Strategy 1: The Division of Responsibility

This is the single most effective approach to feeding children, developed by feeding expert Ellyn Satter. It applies perfectly to the Low-FODMAP diet.

**You decide:**
- What food is offered (low-FODMAP, age-appropriate)
- When food is served (regular meal and snack times)
- Where food is eaten (at the table, without distractions)

**Your child decides:**
- Whether to eat
- How much to eat
- Which foods on their plate to try

Take the pressure off. Your job is to provide safe, nutritious food. Their job is to eat (or not eat) it.

### Strategy 2: Exposure Without Pressure

It takes 10-15 exposures to a new food before a child accepts it. Not tasting — seeing, touching, smelling, and eventually tasting.

**The exposure ladder:**
1. Food is on the table (not on their plate)
2. Food is on their plate (they don't have to eat it)
3. They touch the food with a fork
4. They touch the food with their fingers
5. They lick the food
6. They take a "no-thank-you bite" (one tiny taste)
7. They eat a small portion
8. They eat a normal portion

This can take weeks or months per food. That's normal. Don't rush it.

### Strategy 3: The Safe Food Foundation

Every meal should include at least one food you know your child will eat. This is non-negotiable. If you're serving a Low-FODMAP kids' meal that's new, also put a banana, rice cake, or plain pasta on the plate. The safe food reduces anxiety and ensures your child doesn't go to bed hungry.

### Strategy 4: Involve Them in Cooking

Children are significantly more likely to eat food they helped prepare. Every recipe in this book includes a "Little Chef Task" — a safe, age-appropriate step your child can help with.

**For younger children (3-6):** Washing vegetables, stirring, tearing lettuce, adding pre-measured ingredients
**For older children (7-11):** Measuring ingredients, cracking eggs, setting timers, choosing recipes
**For teens (12+):** Following a recipe independently, meal planning, grocery shopping

### Strategy 5: Don't Offer Alternatives

This is the hardest strategy but the most effective. If your child refuses dinner and you immediately offer a peanut butter sandwich, you've taught them that refusing dinner gets them a preferred food. Instead:

1. Offer a balanced plate with at least one safe food
2. If they refuse, calmly say: "Okay, you don't have to eat. Dinner is at 6 pm tomorrow."
3. No snacks, no alternatives, no special meals for one hour before the next meal
4. Trust that a healthy child will not starve themselves

This only works if you're consistent. One night of caving undoes a week of progress.

### Strategy 6: Use the Traffic Light System with Picky Eaters

Let your child use the traffic light system to identify foods THEY think are safe. This gives them a sense of control. Print the green food list from Chapter 8 and let your child circle the foods they're willing to try this week. Then you cook from their choices.

You'll be surprised at what they pick when they feel in charge.

### When to Worry

Picky eating is normal. But these signs warrant a conversation with your pediatrician:

- Weight loss or failure to gain weight
- Dropping previously accepted foods (more than 5-10 foods lost)
- Extreme anxiety around food (crying, hiding, gagging at the sight of new foods)
- Refusal to eat for more than 24 hours
- Significant nutritional gaps (consult a dietitian)

## Chapter 7: School Lunches, Parties & Eating Out

This is the chapter that will save your sanity. The social aspects of a restricted diet are often harder than the diet itself. We'll cover the three biggest challenges: school lunches, birthday parties, and eating out.

### Part 1: School Lunches

The school lunch is the meal most likely to derail the Low-FODMAP diet. You can't control what your child eats at school. You can't be there to remind them about portion sizes. And your child doesn't want to be different.

**The lunchbox formula:**

Every lunchbox should contain:
1. **Main:** A low-FODMAP protein + grain combo (thermos or cold)
2. **Fruit:** Low-FODMAP options (banana, blueberries, strawberries, orange, grapes — portion-controlled)
3. **Vegetable:** Carrot sticks, cucumber slices, bell pepper strips, cherry tomatoes
4. **Treat:** Something fun (low-FODMAP muffin, cookie, or pudding from Part 6)

**IBS-friendly school lunches that work:**

- **Thermos meals (Chapter 11):** Leftover stir-fry, rice with chicken and carrots, gluten-free pasta with tomato sauce
- **Cold lunches (Chapter 12):** Rice cakes with peanut butter and banana, low-FODMAP wraps with turkey and lettuce, quinoa salad with cucumber and lemon
- **Bento-style (Chapter 12):** Compartmentalized boxes with safe crackers, cheese cubes, carrot sticks, blueberries, and a mini muffin

**Talking to the school:**

Before school starts, schedule a meeting with:
- Your child's teacher
- The school nurse
- The cafeteria manager (if your child buys lunch)

What to say: *"My child has been diagnosed with IBS and is following a medically supervised Low-FODMAP diet to manage their symptoms. Here's what they can and can't eat. I'll be packing their lunch, but I wanted you to be aware in case of classroom celebrations or snack times."*

Bring:
- A one-page summary of the diet
- A list of safe treats (so the teacher knows what to offer during parties)
- An emergency snack pack to keep in the classroom (in case of unexpected treats)

**Classroom celebrations:**

Keep a stash of safe treats in the classroom. When there's a birthday party, the teacher can quietly offer your child something from the stash. Most teachers are happy to do this. Good options: Enjoy Life brand snacks (many are low-FODMAP), individual packs of low-FODMAP crackers with cheese, or prepackaged low-FODMAP cookies.

### Part 2: Birthday Parties

Birthday parties are anxiety triggers for many parents on this diet. Here's a system that works:

**Before the party:**
1. Contact the parent hosting the party. *"My child has a medical diet. I'll send a party pack with them — no need to adjust your menu!"*
2. Pack a PARTY PACK: a small, exciting container with special treats that look party-worthy, not like a sad substitute.
3. The party pack should be MORE appealing than the offered food — not less.

**The party pack formula:**
- A special container (bright color, stickers, or a favorite character)
- 2-3 sweet items (low-FODMAP cupcake, cookies, fruit skewers)
- 1-2 savory items (low-FODMAP pizza, crackers and cheese, chicken nuggets — yes, homemade low-FODMAP chicken nuggets work)
- A small non-food treat (sticker, temporary tattoo, bouncy ball)

**At the party:**
- Brief the parent briefly: "The pack has everything they need. They know what's theirs."
- Remind your child: "You have special party treats in your pack. Enjoy them, and you can skip the cake if you want."
- Don't hover. Let them navigate.

**After the party:**
- Praise your child for handling it well (even if they had a hard moment)
- If they ate something they shouldn't have, don't punish or shame. Note it, check for symptoms, and move on.

### Part 3: Eating Out

Restaurant meals are high-FODMAP minefields. Garlic and onion are in nearly every savory dish. But with the right approach, eating out is possible.

**Choose the right cuisine:**

| Cuisine | Low-FODMAP Friendliness |
|---------|------------------------|
| Sushi (plain rice + fish + vegetables) | ✅ Excellent |
| Grilled meat/seafood + steamed vegetables | ✅ Good (ask for no garlic butter) |
| Mexican — tacos with corn tortillas | ✅ Good (skip beans, check seasoning) |
| Japanese — grilled fish, rice, miso soup | ✅ Good |
| American — burgers (no bun), grilled chicken | ✅ Doable |
| Italian | ❌ Hard (garlic, onion, wheat in everything) |
| Indian | ❌ Very hard (onion/garlic base in curries) |
| Chinese | ⚠️ Difficult (soy sauce, garlic, hidden FODMAPs) |

**Your restaurant script:**

*"I have a child with a medical dietary restriction. I know this makes things complicated, and I appreciate your help."*

Then ask:
1. *"Can you tell me if this dish has onion or garlic?"*
2. *"Can you prepare it without oil that's been infused with garlic?"*
3. *"Can we substitute steamed vegetables for the seasoned ones?"*
4. *"Is there butter on the steak/fish, or can we get it without?"*

**If you're worried about being "that parent":**

Don't be. One in seven people has IBS. Restaurants deal with dietary restrictions all day long. You're not being difficult — you're keeping your child healthy. Most servers and chefs are happy to accommodate when you're polite and appreciative.

**Emergency restaurant kit:**
Keep a small pouch in your bag: individual packs of salt, pepper, olive oil, and a low-FODMAP seasoning blend. That way if plain chicken and rice is your only option, you can dress it up.

### 🔴 RED LIGHT FOODS (Avoid During Elimination)

These foods are high-FODMAP at standard serving sizes. Avoid completely during Phase 1 (elimination). Test individually during Phase 2 (reintroduction).

#### Fruits (Avoid)
Apples, pears, mangoes, watermelon, cherries, peaches, plums, apricots, nectarines, persimmons, lychee, rambutan, dried fruits (all — raisins, dates, figs, prunes, dried apricots, dried mango), fruit juice concentrates, apple juice, pear juice

#### Vegetables (Avoid)
Onion (all varieties: white, yellow, red, green/scallion — white parts only safe), garlic (all forms), leek (white parts), shallots, asparagus, artichoke, cauliflower, mushrooms (all varieties), sugar snap peas, snow peas (in large amounts)

#### Legumes (Avoid)
All beans (kidney, black, pinto, navy, cannellini, soybeans), lentils (all varieties — green, red, brown, black), chickpeas (in large amounts), edamame

#### Grains (Avoid in large amounts)
Wheat bread, wheat pasta, rye bread, barley, couscous, farro, spelt, kamut, semolina — unless certified gluten-free

#### Dairy (Avoid)
Milk (cow, goat, sheep), soft cheeses (ricotta, cottage cheese, mascarpone), yogurt (regular), ice cream, sour cream, condensed milk, evaporated milk, buttermilk

#### Nuts & Seeds (Avoid)
Cashews, pistachios

#### Sweeteners (Avoid)
Honey, agave nectar, high-fructose corn syrup, sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol, isomalt, erythritol (in large amounts)

#### Other (Avoid)
Chicory root fiber, inulin, Jerusalem artichoke, garlic powder, onion powder, garlic salt, onion salt, garlic-infused oil (controversial — some sources say it's safe, Monash says avoid)
